Why Swiggy's food users
never try Instamart

A diagnosis of the cross-vertical adoption gap — why 65% of Swiggy users are stuck on one service, and three specific interventions to close that gap.

AuthorVishnu C
TypeIndependent case study
DomainConsumer App · Growth
Year2026
One
The Problem

Swiggy built the platform.
Users never discovered it.

Swiggy has built something unusual — a platform where you can order dinner, get groceries in 10 minutes, and book a restaurant table, all from one app. They have invested so heavily in Instamart that it now exists both as a feature inside the Swiggy app and as its own standalone app. The intention is clear: Swiggy wants to be present in every moment a user thinks about food — whether cooking at home, ordering in, or dining out. Yet despite this investment, the majority of their food users have never opened Instamart once.

Based on publicly available data, approximately 65% of Swiggy users use only one service. Most of them are food delivery users — they open the app, search for a restaurant, order food, and close it. Instamart is right there, one tap away, but they never try it.

Why this matters

Converting an existing user to a new service costs almost nothing compared to acquiring a brand new user from scratch. If just 10% of Swiggy's food-only users placed one Instamart order in the next 60 days, that would add hundreds of thousands of orders — without spending a single rupee on new user acquisition.

This case study diagnoses why that crossover is not happening and recommends three specific, testable interventions to close the gap.

Two
Metrics

How do we know if it is getting better?

Before diagnosing the problem, we need to define exactly how we will measure success — and what we must not break in the process.

⭐ North Star

Supporting
Second Instamart order within 7 days

🛡 Guardrail

Why the guardrail matters

A user who tries Instamart once because of a discount and never returns is not a win — it is a false signal. The guardrail ensures we are growing the pie, not just moving slices around.

Three
Diagnosis

What actually happens when you open the app?

To understand why food users are not crossing over, I opened the Swiggy app as a regular user and observed the experience firsthand.

The first thing you see when you open Swiggy is food. The homepage is built entirely around food delivery — restaurants, cuisines, offers. Instamart, Dineout, and other services appear as tabs below the address bar, but they require a deliberate tap to reach. A user who opens Swiggy to order food will scroll through restaurants, place an order, and close the app — without ever naturally encountering Instamart.

Key observation

This is not a user behaviour problem. It is a design and placement problem. Users are not ignoring Instamart — they are simply never being shown it at a moment when it is relevant to them.

The only moment Swiggy currently attempts to introduce Instamart to a food user is after they have placed a food order — with a ₹50 discount offer. The intention is right, but the timing is wrong. A user who has just ordered dinner is not thinking about groceries. They are thinking about when their food arrives. The offer gets ignored not because users do not want groceries — but because Swiggy is showing it at the wrong moment.

Three hypotheses

H1 — Low visibility: Most food users never actively notice Instamart because the app never surfaces it to them naturally.

H2 — Wrong trigger moment: Swiggy attempts to convert food users at the least relevant time — right after they have ordered food.

H3 — No habit-building mechanism: A single ₹50 offer can create a one-time transaction, but not a lasting behaviour change.

Four
Recommendations

Three interventions.
Each addresses one hypothesis.

Time-based Instamart surfacing
Addresses H1

Between 7am and 11am, surface Instamart prominently on the homepage — this is when people think about groceries and what to cook. Between 6pm and 8pm, show it again when dinner cooking decisions are being made. Outside these windows, food remains the default.

A/B test: 50% see current homepage. 50% see time-aware homepage surfacing Instamart during morning and evening windows. Primary metric: first Instamart order rate within 30 days.
Cook-it-yourself mode
Addresses H2

When a user searches for a dish — say "chicken noodles" — show a secondary option: "Cook it yourself — get all ingredients delivered in 10 minutes via Instamart." Show the ingredient list, estimated cost, and servings. This turns a food search into a natural Instamart discovery moment.

A/B test: 50% of users searching common home-cookable dishes see the cook-it-yourself option. Primary metric: click-through rate and first Instamart order rate within 14 days.
Instamart add-on at checkout
Addresses H3

When a user confirms their food order, show one relevant Instamart item — a cold drink, dessert, or snack that pairs naturally with what they ordered. "Complete your meal — add a Coke from Instamart, delivered in 10 minutes." This reaches the user when they are already in buying mode.

A/B test: 50% see the Instamart add-on at checkout. Primary metric: add-on conversion rate and second Instamart order within 7 days.
Five
Expected Impact

What success looks like in numbers.

20–25%
of food-only users place first Instamart order within 60 days
₹0
spent on new user acquisition to generate these orders
7 days
the window that tells us if the habit is forming

Combined across the three touchpoints, a conservative estimate is that 20–25% of currently food-only users would place at least one Instamart order within 60 days. At Swiggy's scale, a 20% crossover rate represents hundreds of thousands of new Instamart orders generated without any new acquisition spend.

What to watch in the first 30 days

The most important number after launch is not the 60-day crossover rate — it is the 7-day repeat rate on Instamart. If users come back within a week, the habit is forming. If they do not, the interventions created transactions but not behaviour change — and the approach needs to be reconsidered before scaling.

"The goal is not one Instamart order.
The goal is a user who no longer thinks of Swiggy as only a food app."
